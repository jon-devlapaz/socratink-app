# Dunlosky et al. 2013 — Effective Learning Techniques Monograph

## Summary

Dunlosky, Rawson, Marsh, Nathan, and Willingham (2013) published a 55-page monograph in *Psychological Science in the Public Interest* (14(1), 4–58) rating ten common learning techniques by utility. Utility ratings integrate breadth of evidence across learner ages, materials, criterion tasks, and educational contexts — not raw effect size alone.

Two techniques rated **HIGH utility**: practice testing (retrieval practice, self-testing) and distributed practice (spaced practice). Both generalize broadly across ages, materials, retention intervals, and educational contexts. Both require minimal training to implement.

Three techniques rated **MODERATE utility**: elaborative interrogation, self-explanation, and interleaved practice. Each has demonstrated effects but with meaningful boundary conditions — elaborative interrogation may not transfer to lengthy or complex material and may require prior domain knowledge; interleaving effects depend on task structure; self-explanation evidence is strongest on short-delay associative/conceptual memory.

Five techniques rated **LOW utility**: summarization, highlighting/underlining, keyword mnemonic, imagery use for text, and rereading. The monograph flags that these low-utility techniques are exactly the ones students most commonly self-report using — a learner-self-selection problem that product design can address by steering toward evidence-backed alternatives.

Applicable to socratink: the three-phase drill loop sits on the two highest-utility techniques in the literature (practice testing + distributed practice). Socratic drill prompts touch the moderate tier (elaborative interrogation, self-explanation, interleaving). Rereading, highlighting, and summarization should be actively deprioritized in product flows even when learners request them.
