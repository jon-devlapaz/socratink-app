# Evidence Note — Dunlosky et al. 2013

## Paper

- Citation: Dunlosky, J., Rawson, K. A., Marsh, E. J., Nathan, M. J., & Willingham, D. T. (2013). Improving Students' Learning With Effective Learning Techniques: Promising Directions From Cognitive and Educational Psychology. *Psychological Science in the Public Interest*, 14(1), 4–58.
- Year: 2013
- Domain: Cognitive Psychology / Educational Psychology
- Study type: review / meta-analysis (monograph)
- External file: /Users/jondev/dev/socratink/research/cognitive-psychology/dunlosky2013.pdf
- Brain raw path: .socratink-brain/raw/research-notes/dunlosky2013.md

## Relevance

- Relevant phase(s): phase-2 (retrieval), phase-4 (consolidation). Secondary phase-3 (feedback via practice-testing).
- Why this paper matters for socratink: authoritative utility ranking of 10 learning techniques. Directly adjudicates which drill structures belong in the scaffold (SFT-analog) layer. High-utility techniques (practice testing, distributed practice) are exactly what socratink's three-phase loop implements. Moderate-utility techniques (interleaved practice, elaborative interrogation, self-explanation) map to socratic prompt design. Low-utility techniques (rereading, highlighting, summarization) are what socratink should NOT default to.

## Core Claim

- Main claim in one sentence: Practice testing and distributed practice have high utility for learning across ages, materials, retention intervals, and educational contexts; interleaving, elaborative interrogation, and self-explanation have moderate utility; rereading, highlighting, summarization, keyword mnemonic, and imagery-for-text have low utility.

## Evidence Strength

- Direct evidence: Meta-analytic review across hundreds of studies. Explicit utility ratings with named boundary conditions. Direct for: practice testing ≈ high utility, distributed practice ≈ high utility.
- Indirect evidence: Utility ratings are evidence-backed but depend on researcher interpretation of evidence breadth. Direct effect sizes not re-computed in monograph; it synthesizes extant meta-analyses.
- Speculation or extrapolation: Extension of utility ratings from controlled studies to specific clinical-reasoning / NCLEX-style retrieval is inference, not direct evidence. socratink's specific population (adult nursing learners) is adjacent-but-not-identical to studied populations.

## Findings

- Finding 1: Practice testing received HIGH utility rating. Effects demonstrated across practice-test formats, material kinds, learner ages, outcome measures, and retention intervals. Broad applicability with minimal training cost. Boundary condition flagged: limited research on how benefits vary by learner prior knowledge / ability.
- Finding 2: Distributed practice received HIGH utility rating. Works across ages, materials, standard lab measures, and long delays. Easy to implement (with some training). Demonstrated in classroom studies.
- Finding 3: Interleaved practice, elaborative interrogation, and self-explanation received MODERATE utility. Interleaving benefits depend on task structure; elaborative interrogation limited for learners with low domain knowledge and for lengthy/complex material; self-explanation evidence strongest on associative/conceptual memory at short delays.
- Finding 4 (negative): Rereading, highlighting, summarization, keyword mnemonic, and imagery-for-text received LOW utility. These are the techniques students most commonly self-report using. Implication: learner self-selection of techniques systematically underperforms evidence-backed alternatives.

## Limits

- Population or task limits: Most cited studies use K-12 or undergraduate populations. Adult learners in professional-licensure prep (e.g., NCLEX) are adjacent but not primary target population.
- Ecological validity limits: Monograph authors explicitly flag that many findings come from laboratory tasks; "representative educational contexts" evidence is weaker for some techniques.
- Interpretation limits: Utility ratings integrate breadth-of-evidence with magnitude-of-effect. A HIGH rating does not mean largest effect size — it means most generalizable.
- What this paper does NOT establish: (a) optimal dose/frequency of retrieval practice, (b) interaction effects between techniques, (c) motivation/adherence effects when techniques feel harder, (d) whether reward-layer/gamification interacts with technique efficacy.

## Contradictions Or Tensions

- Conflicts with: common learner intuitions and commercial study-product design (which tend to emphasize rereading, highlighting, summarization).
- Open questions raised: moderators by prior knowledge, boundary conditions for interleaving in clinical reasoning, and adherence under desirable-difficulty conditions.

## Product Implications

- Safe implication: socratink's core loop (cold retrieval + spaced re-drill) sits on the highest-utility evidence-backed foundation in this literature. Scaffold layer is correctly designed. (Direct support.)
- Safe implication: Interleaved practice can be layered into drill ordering for moderate additional benefit. (Direct support, moderate magnitude.)
- Safe implication: socratink should actively steer learners AWAY from rereading-heavy review workflows even when learners self-request them. (Direct support.)
- Risky implication: Claiming socratink is "evidence-based" should cite this monograph explicitly; generalizing to specific NCLEX score outcomes is extrapolation.
- Language we should avoid: "proven to work for nurses", "guaranteed retention improvement", "evidence shows X% score increase" — these overstate the monograph's population and scope.
- Suggested product wording: "drill structure based on two highest-utility learning techniques identified by Dunlosky et al. 2013: practice testing and distributed practice."

## Confidence

- Confidence: high
- Why: monograph is a widely cited authoritative review. Central claims (practice testing + distributed practice = high utility; rereading + highlighting = low utility) are supported by multiple independent meta-analyses. Direct mapping to socratink scaffold mechanisms. Main uncertainty is population-transfer to nursing / NCLEX, which is inference not evidence.

## Reusable Quote Or Paraphrase Notes

- Short paraphrase 1: "Testing effects have been demonstrated across an impressive range of practice-test formats, kinds of material, learner ages, outcome measures, and retention intervals. Thus, practice testing has broad applicability." (Section 8.5, Dunlosky et al. 2013)
- Short paraphrase 2: "We rate distributed practice as having high utility: it works across students of different ages, with a wide variety of materials, on the majority of standard laboratory measures, and over long delays." (Section 9.5, Dunlosky et al. 2013)
- Short paraphrase 3: Elaborative interrogation benefits "may be limited for learners with low levels of domain knowledge" (Section 1.5) — relevant caveat for socratink prompts.
